Representation in the Arts

Adam Herold & Minzie Kim

Adam: We wanted to have a talk about diversity and inclusion in the fine arts industry. Since we’re both artists, we decided to tackle the problem of representation in the art world from two different angles. I’ll be talking about it from a sort of top- down perspective and delving into what the industry itself can do to help, and Minzie here will be looking at it from the bottom-up and figuring out how the people, particularly the artists, can achieve better representation in art. Let’s start with some introductions.

Minzie: I am a Korean-American female student currently majoring in Fine Arts at Cornell. My knowledge from numerous art related classes I took, combined with my own background, gives me some insight and more in-depth views of the art world, compared with those who aren’t as involved. I hope that this knowledge and my experiences as a person of color (POC) female in settings such as art institutions will help people understand more about the inequalities that exist today.

A: And I’m a Jewish guy currently studying Economics and minoring in Fine Art. Having attended both middle school and high school for art, I’ve had the pleasure of growing up and learning with artists from all walks of life.

Being both art and business-minded, I’m hoping to share my two cents on what the industry itself can do to fight for better representation in the art world. So, first things first, let’s take a deeper look at what the problem is from the museums’ perspective.

M: Is it just that they’re not fully committing to better representing different groups of people?

A: Well, that’s definitely part of it, but it goes much deeper than that. I’d like to think that it’s not that the museums aren’t trying to improve. It’s that they’re not taking the right approach or acknowledging that there’s a bigger issue at hand than just ratios in their collections.

M: What do you mean by that?

A: So it’s no secret that a lot of human history was written by a bunch of white dudes who, unsurprisingly, gave other white dudes the limelight in our historical canon. Even though nowadays society has worked to reverse this problem, many artifacts from women or other cultures have been lost to time. And since art history is centered around artifacts, there are more well-preserved and well-represented works from white men in what we consider “art history.”

A: And since representation in art history basically translates to importance, white guys have had way more attention in museums.

M: Yeah I see that. But Eurocentrism is more pervasive in the art world than that. I feel as though that advantage in fame and recognition isn’t limited to museum collections.

A: Oh for sure, this issue’s just as obvious in the art marketplace. Female artists make up a mere 2 percent of the art market, with the top 5 best-selling women making up 40% of that. And the same goes for practically every minority group. Meanwhile, sales are much more evenly distributed for white men, with the top five only accounting for 10%.

M: Isn’t that largely because of the under- representation that you mentioned before?

A: That’s definitely a big part of it, but I also think it’s partly due to the “superstar effect,” where a few well-known artists generate an insane amount of attention at the cost of everyone else. But when you look past these giants at the overarching industry trends, you realize that the art marketplace is just as skewed as it has always been.

M: So then how should museums and auction houses go about solving this problem?

A: I think the best thing to do is give lesser- known artists the spotlight.

M: But that goes completely against how they usually function. Business is usually driven by name recognition and historical importance.

A: But here’s the thing, a recent exhibition by the Guggenheim shows that doing so might not be as backwards as it seems. Have you heard about the Klint exhibit?

M: I’ve heard of it vaguely, but never bothered to look into it.

A: So back in 2018, the Gugg dedicated nearly all of its wall space to the female artist Hilma Af Klint, a total outsider to the mainstream art canon. Yet despite going completely against the idea that the more well-known the artist, the more successful the show, this ended up being their most-attended exhibition ever. A lot of this exhibit’s success might be due to the fact that as a society, we are beginning a push to represent the under-represented.

M: So what you’re suggesting is that maybe the public doesn’t care so much about the “superstars” as much as we might think? And that maybe people want to start looking at paintings by more than just the top five white men?

A: Exactly! Not only does it seem to be great for business, but it seems like the perfect way to begin re-imagining the art history.

M: While I do agree with you that museums have some responsibility to expand representation in the art industry, isn’t it also important for the artists and viewers themselves to take advantage of existing systems to expand representation, like female-only or POC-only galleries, to accomplish these goals?

A: That seems like a pretty good way for these artists to get exposure. It’s like the solution I mentioned previously about giving lesser-known artists the spotlight.

M: It is! But there’s a problem with artists not wanting to participate in such galleries.

A: Why’s that?

M: Well, a lot under-represented artists are scared of “ghettoization”, or being sanctioned into a specific space based on their identity.

A: So, for example, an artist exhibiting in a female-only gallery might be afraid of being labeled as a “female artist” rather than just an artist?

M: Exactly. Or an African-American artist may refuse to contribute their works to a Black Pride Month exhibition out of fear that their work will be labeled solely by their race and thus be excluded from the mainstream art world, which is predominantly white and male.

A: Oh I totally see that, their chance to showcase in one location would limit their chances to showcase in another, creating a double bind.

M: Exactly! Currently, there is an overzealous demand for art by female POC just for the diversity factor. Often, their race and gender identities, rather than their identity as artists, are taken into account when curating their work.

A: Doesn’t that create an opportunity for people to profit off their identity, rather than their art?

M: Yeah, it’s not great. Instead, artists should be using their identities to help them meaningfully innovate their art, instead of just using their under- represented status to foster this current wave.

A: Okay that’s way more reasonable. So you think that despite the negative connotations of taking advantage of your what makes you yourself, artists should take advantage of this system still?

M: Yeah, I believe that whether it be population specific exhibitions or mainstream museums, artists can’t escape being labeled by their identity. It’s just a question of how you take advantage of that “name tag.”

A: And how would they take advantage? It seems that the system is already skewed against them.

M: If they get labeled regardless of these exhibitions, artists should just simply start by showcasing their art in these “binding” exhibitions. Mainstream museums are going to be hard to get in anyways, and building up your reputation and exposure to the professional art world is important.

A: That’s definitely a solid strategy for finding your footing in mainstream media.

M: As a POC female artist myself, I feel as though the “label” mentioned before is a necessary sacrifice we have to make in order to gain access to mainstream exposure.

When we’re finally “inside” the mainstream art world, we can get rid of the labels. Because if this trend of specialized exhibitors for under-represented groups continues, I think that the value of POC and female artists will gradually be integrated to the art industry.

A: Hmm.. obviously that’s not ideal, but I agree. As more artists emerge and participate in more and more exhibitions, I think it’ll be a totally new wave in the art industry. Maybe one day, we’ll care about the artwork and the artist themselves, without getting hung up on labeling their identities.

M: I hope so! But it’ll take as much change from the bottom-up, the artists themselves, as it will from the top-down, the museums and the curators.
