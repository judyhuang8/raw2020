Triple A

Mollie Zhang
Diversity and Inclusion or Diversity VS Inclusion?

It started with a conversation among the thought- curators on the Editorial team. After Colton’s proposal of the “donut” diagram of diversity as the outer ring and inclusion as the inner ring, we discovered that inclusivity may be a cost to achieving higher diversity. Is it possible to enhance diversity AND inclusion simultaneously in our writing?

To answer this question, the thought-curation team designed an experiment. The editorial team was split into two separate groups to compete against each other, and each had to design a recruitment process for “Triple A multimedia design fraternity.” However, the two teams did not know that they were working on different prompts: one team was designing a selective and diverse recruitment process, while the other team was focusing on a selective and inclusive process.

The two teams were given three weeks to develop their recruitment process before presenting in front of the entire team. We revealed the difference in their prompts right before they presented to preserve the independence of their design thinking. The following will point out some major differences between the two design processes.

Definition of inclusivity and diversity:
The inclusivity team defined inclusivity as “communication, compromising, understanding, and willingness to include.” The diversity team emphasized diversity in people’s skills. They expect their “Triple A multimedia design fraternity” brothers to be experts in diverse areas of design.

Written Application

The two teams both chose to use written application as their first stage of the recruitment process.

The applicants would submit an application form, and the brothers in the organization will evaluate based on their responses. The two teams’ justification for this choice is as follow:

The inclusive team:
To evaluate the applicant’s passion while giving both potential and mature designers equal opportunity, the inclusive team decided to pick a variety of prompts and ask the applicants to critique three pieces of their choice. The team provides a total of nine prompts in three categories: branding identity, photography, and CAD.

The diverse team:
The diverse team aimed to design an application process that classifies the applicants for them and builds a basis for selecting equally across diverse design fields. Thus, their application requires applicants not only to respond to a prompt, but also requires them to submit three or more past designs reflecting their strength.

Interview Process

For the next stage, both teams chose to interview the applicants that theoretically passed the first round of application. Both teams were designing under the assumption that the time needed to train interviewers and conducting interviews could be ignored. This was the most interesting stage: the two designs visibly diverged and either achieved diversity of final interviewees or inclusivity of the interviewees.

The inclusive team:
The inclusive team designed a two-part interview process to fully test whether the interviewee possesses the quality of being inclusive. Interviewees are paired together based on their selection in the first round. After their discussion, participants are first given a few minutes to individually redesign the prompt that they have picked.

Then, they will communicate with each other and come up with a final redesign that incorporates both of their opinions. Eventually, the evaluators will make the decisions based on both the quality of their collaborative design and their skills to incorporate others’ opinions into their designs.

The diverse team:
The diverse team designed a thorough interview process that evaluates both the participants’ skill set and their characteristics. One round of the interview aims to mainly evaluate their ability to make good design, while the other is composed of mainly behavioral questions for vibe check. However, both their critical skills and compatibility with the group’s vibe are being evaluated in each round, with weights of 75% and 25%. The interviewees do not know about the double evaluation rubric. This ensures that the interviewers can observe the participant’s most natural behavior and make a fairer judgment.

Conclusion

The two teams’ recruitment process followed the traditional route: application, interview, and final-round cut. However, their emphasis on the candidates’ desired qualities are different. The diverse team ensures a new pledge class of people with distinct design skills, while the inclusive team implemented three rounds of interviews to evaluate the applicant’s communication skills and inclusive mindset. Aiming to achieve both diversity and inclusivity, we hope to use the insights we learned from this experiment to build a more diverse and inclusive recruitment process for Medium in the future.

Design is not limited to spaces and products- but also processes. Re-imagine a process (ex: job application) to make it more diverse and inclusive.
