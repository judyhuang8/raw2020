Why My Chair is Not Comfy

Jennifer Lee
The Issue with Ergonomics

The design process of ergonomics focuses on arranging the workplace, products, and systems so that they optimize the health, safety, and comfort of the user. Historically, the design process has been targeted to benefit an ideal or primary user. But how do designers decide who the ideal user is, and how does this decision affect everyone else who isn’t ideal? What happens when one size does not fit all?

Deriving from the work setting, ergonomics has been traditionally focused on the ideal office worker, someone who remained stationary for hours and hours. Moreover, this ideal office worker, in terms of body size and proportions, is often a middle-aged white male. But what about every other identity factor (body-type, gender, age, handicaps, etc.)

that may prevent an individual from being a traditionally ideal user? The way we are designing and arranging our office spaces is clearly not inclusive of all identities and human backgrounds.

The question remains, however, as to how we can move to address design biases in ergonomics. How can we change the design focus of ergonomic products from an ideal user in order to accommodate all users? How can we make ergonomics comfortable and efficient for all?

A publication by the American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees (AFSCME) explores the “Injuries Caused by Poor Ergonomics.” Although there are no immediate effects of poor ergonomics, long-term exposure to uncomfortable working conditions can cause a variety of injuries and illnesses, including carpal tunnel syndrome, ganglion cysts, and Raynaud’s Syndrome.

These maladies stem from the fact that what is comfortable for a tall, average-sized male might put unnecessary strain on a shorter, more slender user, for instance. And because they happen over long periods of time and are hard to observe, these discomforts have persisted in the workplace.

Product Design Gone Wrong

Designing products around the needs of an ideal user is not limited to the office; in fact, it can be more easily observed in an industry such as pharmaceuticals, where doses of painkillers and sleep medications aimed at an ideal user might have immediate repercussions for everyone else.

Developing and testing the long-term effects of new pharmaceutical products can cost millions of dollars. In an effort to cut costs, most products are created using data from white males, simply because there has historically been more information obtained about this demographic than any of the others.

For example, the average age at which autism is diagnosed in males is 3.8, while the average age is 7.6 in females. This four-year discrepancy is purely due to the fact that the large majority of data points obtained from observing autism have been focused on young boys.

Even more startling is the poor ergonomic design of the three-point seat belt system in motor vehicles.

A paper by Bose et al. in 2011 found that female drivers were almost 50% more likely to sustain injuries during moderate to severe crashes than their male counterparts, a direct consequence of designing seatbelts for an ideal male user.

Hope for a Comfier Future

Designers and engineers alike are beginning to understand the need to tailor their products so that they work for every user, not simply the “ideal” ones. The future of design is interdisciplinary, human-centric, and self-accountable.

Randy Hunt, head of Design and Grab, says that “2020 will be a tipping point for designers taking responsibility for their work.”

In our decade, we are beginning to see the world of design trending towards ethical product design. Designers are beginning to consider the context of their products: What contribution does the product provide? Who does the product help? How will the product function in the long-term? Many companies are also partnering with ethical, values-driven organizations to ensure that their materials are responsibly sourced, and that their products introduce good behaviors and optimal user experiences.

One such company that exemplifies the principles of ethical design is Humane by Design, created by Jon Yablonski. Humane by Design creates products that actually help its users.

These are ethical and intelligent designs, aware that different users may have different needs and different abilities. They respect the time and attention of their users, and prioritize user experience over revenue.

The movement in the design community toward ethical and humane design illustrates that the world is making positive strides toward inclusive design practices. The days of the ideal user are rapidly retreating. Because in the end, every user should be an ideal user. Regardless of our identities, we all deserve to have products that make us feel comfortable and safe. And with the current trend in the design community toward inclusive design, soon, we will all be able to sit in a comfy chair of our own.
