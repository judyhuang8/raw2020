Diversity & Inclusion in Architecture

Desai Wang

Starchitects—famous, star-like, architects—and their architecture dominate our time. Frank Gehry, Eero Saarinen, I.M. Pei and Rem Koolhaas are just some amongst an ever-growing list. Perhaps you don’t recognize their names, but you may have heard of the Guggenheim Museum in Bilbao, Glass Pyramids at the Louvre, Gateway Arch in St. Louis, or the CCTV tower in Beijing. Starchitecture dots the world, Cornell included: I.M. Pei designed the Johnson Museum of Art and Rem Koolhaas, Milstein Hall.

Bold and imaginative designs characterize these high-profile, high-budget works, making them perfect for magazine covers and city promotions. Not only are works from starchitects guaranteed to look good, they have also become status symbols. Yet, forms aside, they accomplish little.

Many high-profile designs are advertised to be “sustainable” when experimental forms often contradict sustainability. For example, Milstein Hall has a green roof and is certified Gold by LEED (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design). However, Cornell architecture professor Johnathan Ochshorn points out that it is “a sealed glass box... that maximizes weather-exposed surfaces” and “uses far more building materials than needed.” Further, Ochshorn notes that many features which helped Milstein secure the gold rating had little to do with the architectural form itself. For example, features that earned LEED points include water- efficient plumbing fixtures and dirt-capturing entry mats, which are more related to interior elements than the building design.

In other words, Milstein Hall, along with most starchitecture, is not as sustainable as it claims to be.

While people, me included, are wowed by the big firms and their dazzling designs, I think we have a responsibility to look beyond forms. Industrialization has created new materials and new methods of construction, but having the ability to shape building in new ways does not mean we have to.

Technological advancement does not result in invincibility. The location of a building, and the needs of people inhabiting it, should be prioritized over the architectural form. Architecture should reflect the diversity of its location and be inclusive towards the people it serves. Form should follow function.

Burj Khalifa, a skyscraper of 828 meters, should not exist in Dubai regardless of its impressive height and form. The hot and humid climate is the worst place to build an exposed, steel-ribbed glass structure. Its peak electrical demand is 36mW, which equals to 360,000 bulbs of 100 watts operating simultaneously, and its water demand averages to 250,000 gallons per day.

However, that is not to say we should not have high-rises or that they cannot have aesthetically interesting forms. Singapore based firm WOHA demonstrates continued experimentation on the intersection of urban density and sustainability— they ask the question of “how can a metropolis high-rise maximize comfort living and still remain energy efficient?”

WOHA is especially known for their use of green

walls. Unlike the sparse grass that occupies Milstein Hall’s roof, WOHA buildings often incorporate living landscapes into their building design. Founders Wo Mun Summ and Richard Hassell say that their key principle is to design buildings that breathe. They want to design buildings that are “porous” and provide “air movement, buildings that respond to the environment rather than acting as sealed glass boxes”.

In the United States, buildings account for 48% of total energy consumption. Though there is a trend for more sustainable housing, we still have much work to do. While mechanical equipment can help to reduce energy demands, it is important that architecture goes beyond being impressive starchitecture and responds to the environment through its design.
