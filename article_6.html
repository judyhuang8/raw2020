Fast Fashion: Why We Should Be Slowing Down

Ariya Feng

I used to buy shirts at Forever21 for less than $10 thinking it was a steal. Indeed it was a steal. I stole from the lives of women working for less than $3 a day in factories of hazardous working conditions, from the men who spend countless hours in polluted cotton fields hand-picking the plants, and from the environment - a sink that we use to dump our emissions and wastes in. The harsh realities of the horrors that come with consumerism didn’t hit me until I watched “The True Cost” on Netflix, a documentary that depicts the cruelty of the fast fashion industry.

Clothes are a way for people to express who they are, where they come from, and what they represent. So it’s hard when an environmentalist is also a fashion enthusiast. We want to follow trends, but the fast fashion industry creates new trends every week. Old clothes are constantly being thrown out and incinerated, and with it, the laborious hours and environmental damage that went into making it so cheap. We always hear “you are what you eat”, but I believe that “you are what you wear” is an issue that is just as prevalent. What is the solution?

Slow fashion is a fashion approach that opposes the fast fashion industry. Like the pre-industrial revolution, slow fashion focuses on garments that are locally sourced and produced. While the prices are not as “affordable” as fast fashion clothing, people making the clothes are paid a fair wage, and the clothes usually last a lot longer.

Second to only the oil industry, the fashion industry is the second-largest polluter in the world, and is responsible for 10% of global carbon emissions (McFall-Johnson, 2019). The mass production of inexpensive clothes through which companies to keep up with new fashion trends is a large contributor to that. Most fast fashion companies imitate weekly fashion trends as they emerge, creating an enormous quantity and variety of clothing so consumers are always able to find something current to buy. This rapid production comes at the expense of quality materials, the environment, and human well-being. Every year, 80 billion pieces of new clothing are bought, the majority of which are assembled in China and Bangladesh. Yet, the United States consumes more clothing than any other nation (Bick et al, 2018).

Although the fast fashion industry offers consumers clothing at affordable prices, the brief materialistic happiness of those consumers is not worth the pain and suffering of factory workers in developing countries, nor the environmental costs that come with cheap manufacturing. We must stop the constant cycle of buying and disposing clothes and look towards sustainable fashion options.

Affordable Clothing

Advocates of the fast fashion industry claim that in addition to giving consumers a chance to wear similar clothes from the catwalk, garments sold at reasonable prices are affordable for low-income households. In a world where many opportunities are determined based on one’s socioeconomic status, fast fashion offers something for people of all backgrounds.

Another argument is that fast fashion enables people to express themselves, but shopping from sustainable brands is usually a luxury that most cannot afford. Additionally, sustainable brands potentially limit the clothing options that are available to consumers. Fast fashion also increases globalization and stimulates the economy in developing countries when Western retailers employ their labor forces. However, we must also consider the long term societal and environmental impacts.

Environmental Impact

The materials and processes that go into making cheap clothing generates irreversible damage to the environment.

The first step in the global textile supply chain is to manufacture materials, a process that encompasses the production of both natural and synthetic fibers (Bick et al, 2018). The most widely used manufactured fiber, polyester, is made from petroleum. Fast fashion has increased the need for man-made fibers like polyester, with its demand nearly doubling in the past 15 years (Luz, 2007). Not only is the creation of polyester and other synthetic fabrics very energy-intensive, it can also emit “volatile organic compounds, particulate matter, and acid gases... which can cause or aggravate respiratory disease” (Luz, 2007). Textile manufacturing is detrimental to the health of the people in the areas in which they are manufactured, predominantly in developing countries where labor is outsourced. This leads to great inequalities in health and quality of life.

These detrimental emissions are not limited to air pollution. Textile dyeing results in further hazards as untreated wastewater from dyes are dispensed into local water systems. Chemicals including heavy metals and other toxins are harmful to local residents as well as organisms and animals (Bick et al, 2018). Moreover, the most common natural fiber, cotton, is a highly water intensive plant. The fashion industry is the second-largest consumer of the world’s water supply. In fact, approximately 2,000 gallons of water is required to produce one pair of jeans (McFall-Johnson, 2019). In addition to using large amounts of water, mass-production of cotton also requires the use of pesticides, which often ends up discharged in wastewater as well.

Another contributor to carbon emissions is the ever-expanding disposal of clothes.

This can be attributed to fast fashion’s shockingly cheap prices, as well as consumers’ single-use attitudes with clothing. Top fast fashion retailers sell garments that are expected to be worn less than ten times before their disposal (Birstwistle et al, 2007). Growth of the economy is starting to depend on getting rid of old clothes to make room for the new clothes. In addition, constant changes in stylistic norms support continuous discarding as “old styles” are deemed undesirable and useless (Claudio, 2007). Every year, almost 3.8 billion pounds of clothing are sent to landfills as solid waste, which translates to 80 pounds of landfill waste per American per year for disposed clothing alone (Bick et al, 2018).

Municipal solid waste landfills accounted for about 16 percent of U.S. methane (a non-CO2 gas that plays a part in climate change) emissions in 2016 and are the third-largest source of human-related methane emissions (EPA, 2017). As can be seen, clothing choices and disposal rate is closely tied to the wellbeing of the planet.

Worker Impact with Case Studies in Bangladesh

Fashion consumption soared by 60% between 2000 and 2014 alone. In order to keep up with the increasing demand of consumers, fast fashion companies look to low-cost labor as the solution (Remy et al, 2016). Much of this labor are women in developing countries, making less than a dollar an hour. Currently, one in six workers in the world are employed in the fashion industry, 80% of whom are females living in countries such as China, Bangladesh, Vietnam, Sri Lanka, Cambodia and Indonesia (McCosker, 2019). Since the factory production of clothing is in offshore facilities, workers rights are often violated, with little to no regulation of policies pertaining to the workplace. Wages are often so low that low income workers cannot refuse overtime.

Many work 14 to 16 hours a day, 7 days a week. Other concerns includes child labor, and horrendous health and safety hazards. Bangladesh exports more garments than any other country except China. A report found that despite the country’s labor laws, impoverished children under the age of 14 are working an average of 64 hours a week (Beaubin, 2016). Researchers who surveyed households in the slums of Dhaka, the capital and largest city in Bangladesh, found that children as young as six years old were employed full-time, with the average child earning less than $2 a day (Quattri et al., 2016). Two-thirds of the girls working were in the garment industry. The fast fashion industry indirectly plays a role in the deprivation of educational opportunities for these children, which are crucial in escaping the poverty cycle.

Moreover, the buildings in which these garments are mass-produced (by children and women) are commonly kept in hazardous and inhumane conditions. Since 2006, more than 500 Bangladeshi workers have died in factory fires that could have been avoided if appropriate precautions were taken (Bajaj, 2012). Several factories do not have sufficient amounts of fire escapes. While the 1911 Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire led to improved industrial protections and work standards in the United States, this was not the case for countries like Bangladesh, which rely on the vast quantity of low-paid workers for economic growth.

Near Dhaka in 2013, Rana Plaza, an eight-story complex of clothing factories collapsed.

It was the deadliest garment-factory accident in history, with deaths totaling to 1,127 people, consisting of mostly young women (Taplin, 2014). The building was constructed three stories taller than designed and licensed for. Prior to the collapse, factory workers had voiced concerns about the structural safety of the building after noticing cracks on the walls (Hobson, 2013). The factory’s owners did nothing in response. After Rana Plaza’s collapse and the arrest of its owners, thousands of workers protested against poor safety standards (Manik et al., 2013). Despite their efforts, little has been done to heed the fundamental needs and standards of many factory workers, and some protestors are even persecuted. Aminul Islam, a union organizer who fought for higher wages and better working conditions was found tortured and killed outside Dhaka (Bajaj, 2012).

The practice of fast fashion facilitates these “safety shortcuts” (Taplin, 2014). Countries that are rich in labor but poor in capital adapted to a model of production designed to reduce the time needed from design to delivery. Pressure is put on both suppliers to meet delivery deadlines as well as factory owners to keep costs low enough so the retail companies do not turn to other factories that can make the same thing for a cheaper price. The cut in costs come from labor workers’ already barely livable paycheck. Employees in Bangladesh are among the world’s lowest paid people, at a government-mandated rate of $37 a month for entry-level workers (Bajaj, 2012). Fast fashion runs on the cost of their workers’ wellbeing and lives because increasing demand leaves them few other options.

Sustainable Approach

The convenient and cheap aspects of fast fashion often draw the attention of many consumers. However, the processes sacrifice the environment and those laboring in offshore factories. Consumers should be looking to other options to gain the same benefits fast fashion provides. Slow fashion is a good mindset to adopt and encourage in others. A leading brand in slow fashion today is Reformation— they have been carbon-neutral since 2015 and are relatively transparent in their sustainability practices when compared to fast fashion retailers. However, a dress at Reformation can go up to $300, whereas a similar dress at H&M could be purchased for less than $30. Understandably, not everyone is able to contribute to the emerging slow fashion industry and their costly price tags. For those who have the means to, they should. However, sustainable options do not stop here.

The most sustainable route would be to stop consuming altogether. How many pairs of jeans does one really need? Before making purchasing decisions, people should think about what they already have in their closet and the necessity of the product they are buying. Nonetheless, fashion plays a huge role in some people’s lives—sustainability does not have to stop that. To respond to the argument that fast fashion allows more choices, sustainable shopping is not only limited to the few slow fashion brands out there. A great substitute for fast fashion is thrifting. Thrift stores sell second-hand clothing items at a fraction of the original price. Not only are customers saving money when they buy pre-owned clothing, they are also lessening the amount of textile waste that gets dumped in landfills. Furthermore, clothing at thrift stores are usually more unique than mass-produced generic pieces sold by fast fashion retailers, which can make even more of a fashion statement.

Conclusion

The fast fashion industry does more harm than good. Yes, it is convenient and provides clothing at cheap prices, but it comes with irreversible environmental damage and the impaired livelihoods of laborers in developing countries. Consumers do not see everything that happens behind the scenes—depleting water supply, contaminated oceans, collapsed buildings, toxic air, and the many lives lost. Consider reducing consumption of clothing, and thoroughly evaluate whether a new piece of clothing is necessary. For people who use fashion as their way of expression, there are still ways to acquire new clothes while being sustainable. If people are not able to afford slow fashion brands, they can purchase pre-owned clothing items, giving them a new life while reducing textile waste. “Every dollar you spend...or don’t spend ... is a vote you cast for the world you want” (L.N. Smith). Dress and shop responsibly.

Sustainable solutions are often more expensive because product reuse and durability reduces buying frequency and hence, decreases business income. Design a solution that can make sustainable products more affordable.
